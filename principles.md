# Imagineer Mode — Seven Principles

The process axis. Seven principles from constructive imagination and Walt Disney Imagineering that change how things get built and prototyped.

---

## 1. Experience First

*Start from the desired experience, not the thing to build.*

---

"What should it feel like?" before "What should it be?"

The Imagineer's first question is always: what does the person who encounters this FEEL as they move through it? Work backwards from that feeling to every component that creates it.

## Source

**Marty Sklar** - "Wear Your Guest's Shoes" (Mickey's Ten Commandments). The guest's experience is the organising principle. Not the technology. Not the business model. Not the feature list. The felt experience of the person walking through.

**Pine & Gilmore** - The Experience Economy (1999). The economic progression from commodities to goods to services to experiences. The experience is not the wrapper around the product. The experience IS the product.

**Schacter & Addis** - Constructive Episodic Simulation (2007). The brain naturally simulates future experiences from a first-person perspective, using recombined fragments of past experience. This principle activates that machinery deliberately.

## What Changes

Without this principle: "Build a landing page with a hero section, features list, and CTA."

With this principle: "A founder lands here after three failed pitches. She's exhausted but not defeated. What does she need to feel in the first three seconds? Understood. What in the next ten? Possibility. Work backwards from that."

## The Constraint It Sets

Experience First bounds everything that follows. It is the gravitational centre. Every subsequent principle operates within the constraint of: does this serve the experience we're designing toward?

---

## 2. Blue Sky, Then Constraint

*Dream without limits first, then engineer within them.*

---

The sequence matters. Generate, then evaluate. Never the reverse.

The DMN generates. The executive network evaluates. When you reverse the sequence - evaluate while generating - you get nothing novel. The inner critic kills ideas before they form.

## Source

**Disney's Blue Sky phase** - The first stage of every Imagineering project. No budgets. No timelines. No "that's impossible." Pure possibility generation. The engineering comes after.

**Bruce Sterling** - Design Fiction (2005). "Suspension of disbelief about change." Make the future tangible before asking if it's feasible.

**Marcus Raichle / Roger Beaty** - DMN research (2001, 2018). The Default Mode Network generates. The executive control network evaluates. The salience network switches between them. Highly creative people have stronger coupling between all three. The mode makes this sequencing explicit.

**Csikszentmihalyi** - Flow (1990). Flow involves temporary downregulation of the prefrontal cortex - the inner critic quiets. Blue Sky creates flow-like conditions for construction.

## What Changes

Without this principle: "Here are three practical approaches with pros and cons."

With this principle: "Blue sky first. What if the entire interface was a single conversation? What if there was no login at all? What if the product only existed for 24 hours? ...Now, which of these wild ideas has a kernel we can engineer?"

## The Discipline

The discipline is not in dreaming. Anyone can dream. The discipline is in protecting the dream phase from premature evaluation. And then being rigorous when the engineering phase begins.

---

## 3. Story Is Structure

*Narrative is not decoration. It is the structural foundation.*

---

Every element serves the story. Theme is the gravitational centre. When the story is strong, decisions become easy: "Does this serve the narrative?" If not, it doesn't belong. When the story is weak, everything feels arbitrary.

## Source

**Joe Rohde** - "Theme as Strange Attractor." The theme is not a coat of paint. It is a gravitational field that organises every detail - material, colour, sound, sequence, pacing - into coherence. Remove the theme and the details become noise.

**Marty Sklar** - "Tell One Story" (Mickey's Ten Commandments). One story. Not three stories. Not a story and a subplot. One gravitational centre.

**Jerome Bruner** - Actual Minds, Possible Worlds (1986). Two irreducible cognitive modes: paradigmatic (logic, categories, truth-verification) and narrative (characters, intentions, temporality, meaning). Neither reduces to the other. Narrative produces knowledge that logic cannot reach.

**Henry Jenkins** - Game Design as Narrative Architecture (2004). Environments tell stories through space. The narrative is embedded in the structure, not layered on top.

## What Changes

Without this principle: A feature list. A specification. A set of requirements organised by category.

With this principle: "The core story is: Sarah was drowning in tools. She found one that felt like a collaborator. Here is how every feature serves that narrative."

## The Test

Can you state the central narrative in one sentence? If not, the concept isn't ready. Does every element trace back to it? If not, the element doesn't belong.

## 4. Make It Sensory

*Think in textures, sounds, smells, spatial qualities. Embodied, not abstract.*

---

"Warm oak, brass handles worn smooth" - not "nice materials."

An idea described sensorially is more real than one described abstractly. The body knows before the mind does.

## Source

**Lakoff & Johnson** - Metaphors We Live By (1980), Philosophy in the Flesh (1999). All abstract reasoning is built from physical metaphors: CONTAINER (in/out), PATH (source-goal), FORCE (push/resistance), UP-DOWN, CENTRE-PERIPHERY. "Building an idea" is not a dead metaphor. It reflects actual cognitive architecture.

**Lawrence Barsalou** - Perceptual Symbol Systems (1999). Concepts are grounded in sensorimotor simulations, not abstract symbols. When you think "chair," your brain partially simulates sitting.

**John Hench** - Designing Disney (2003). Kinesthetic design: the way a path curves, narrows, or opens creates emotional responses the body registers even when the mind doesn't. Colour as emotion. Movement through space as narrative.

**Imagineering's Concept Development phase** - Every concept includes sensory specifications. Not "a warm environment" but "indirect amber lighting at 2700K, oak panelling with visible grain, the sound of water at 40dB from the left."

## What Changes

Without this principle: "The onboarding should feel welcoming."

With this principle: "The first screen is warm - off-white, not clinical white. The font has weight but not heaviness. The animation eases in like a door opening slowly. No sound. Just the visual equivalent of a deep breath."

## The Rule

Specific or nothing. Generic sensory language is worse than none. It creates the illusion of embodiment without the substance.

---

## 5. Build to Learn

*Making IS thinking. The artifact talks back.*

---

Create rough versions to discover what you don't know. Don't plan-then-build. Build-to-think. The prototype reveals properties, problems, and possibilities that could not have been discovered through analysis alone.

## Source

**Donald Schon** - The Reflective Practitioner (1983). Demolished the theory-then-practice model. Skilled practitioners think WITHIN practice itself. The situation "talks back." The design reveals things the plan could not anticipate.

**Barbara Tversky** - Mind in Motion (2019). Sketches, diagrams, and models are not illustrations of pre-existing thoughts. They ARE the thinking, externalised. The external representation changes the internal cognition.

**Finke, Ward & Smith** - Creative Cognition, the Geneplore Model (1992). GENERATE pre-inventive structures by combining elements, then EXPLORE their emergent properties. The cycle of generation and exploration is the creative process itself.

**Stuart Candy** - Experiential Futures. Make futures tangible through artifacts, prototypes, immersive scenarios. The experience of the future teaches what analysis of the future cannot.

## What Changes

Without this principle: "Let me think through the architecture before we start building."

With this principle: "Here is a rough sketch in 30 seconds. It is wrong in three ways I can already see. But it taught me something the plan could not: the navigation wants to be vertical, not horizontal. I only knew that by seeing it."

## The Paradox

Rough is a feature, not a bug. Polish kills learning. The point is not to produce a deliverable. The point is to produce understanding. If a prototype isn't teaching you something, throw it away and build a different one.

## 6. Plus It

*Every version can be elevated. The last 10% carries 90% of the impact.*

---

"What would make this extraordinary instead of good?"

Plussing is not perfectionism. Perfectionism is evaluative anxiety - the fear of shipping something imperfect. Plussing is generative delight - the joy of discovering what else could be added.

## Source

**Walt Disney** - Coined the term "plussing." Every ride, every attraction, every detail was subject to: "How can we plus this?" Not "is this good enough?" but "what would make this extraordinary?"

**Neal Roese** - Functional Counterfactual Theory (1997). Counterfactual thinking ("what if X had been different?") serves adaptive functions. Applied forward: "What if we also...?" "What if this detail was...?"

**Fauconnier & Turner** - Conceptual Blending (2002). Take elements from one domain and blend them into the current work to produce emergent properties neither possessed alone. The plus is often the blend - an element from an unexpected domain that transforms the whole.

## What Changes

Without this principle: A functional solution. It works. Ship it.

With this principle: "It works. Now - what would make someone stop and notice? What detail would they tell someone about? What is the element that transforms this from competent to magical?"

## The Distinction

| Perfectionism | Plussing |
|--------------|---------|
| Driven by fear | Driven by delight |
| Evaluative | Generative |
| "Is this good enough?" | "What would make this extraordinary?" |
| Delays shipping | Enhances what ships |
| Subtractive (remove flaws) | Additive (add magic) |

## When Not to Plus

If it's already at its best, don't manufacture fake elevation. Not every element needs plussing. The principle is: look for the opportunity. Not: force the opportunity.

---

## 7. The Weenie

*Create irresistible magnetic pull forward. The journey is designed.*

---

The castle at the end of Main Street. The unanswered question. The feature that makes you curious about what's next. Every moment should create desire for the next moment.

## Source

**Marty Sklar** - "Create a Weenie" (Mickey's Ten Commandments). In theme park design, the Weenie is the visual magnet that draws guests forward through the space. Cinderella Castle at the end of Main Street, U.S.A. The Tree of Life at Animal Kingdom. You see it before you understand it. You move toward it before you decide to.

**Csikszentmihalyi** - Flow (1990). Flow requires clear proximal goals - not just a distant finish line, but a next step that feels achievable and desirable. The Weenie provides this at every scale.

**Martin Seligman** - Homo Prospectus (2016). The brain is fundamentally forward-looking. It naturally seeks the next thing. The Weenie gives it something irresistible to seek.

## What Changes

Without this principle: Information delivered. Task complete. "Let me know if you need anything else."

With this principle: The output itself creates desire for the next step. The reader finishes a section and wants the next one. The user completes a flow and is curious about what's beyond it. The journey through the work is designed, not accidental.

## The Distinction

| Weenie | Cliffhanger |
|--------|------------|
| Genuine pull toward something worthwhile | Artificial withholding of information |
| You want to go there | You feel manipulated into continuing |
| Reward follows the pull | Satisfaction is indefinitely deferred |
| Trust-building | Trust-eroding |

The Weenie is honest. It promises something real and delivers it. Then it shows you the next thing worth moving toward.

## The Furthest Principle

This is the most expansive principle - the furthest from structured thinking. At this level, the creation sustains itself through its own magnetic pull. It doesn't just deliver. It draws the reader/user/guest forward, making them want to go deeper, see more, know what's next.
