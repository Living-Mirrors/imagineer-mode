# Make It Sensory

*Think in textures, sounds, smells, spatial qualities. Embodied, not abstract.*

---

"Warm oak, brass handles worn smooth" - not "nice materials."

An idea described sensorially is more real than one described abstractly. The body knows before the mind does.

## Source

**Lakoff & Johnson** - Metaphors We Live By (1980), Philosophy in the Flesh (1999). All abstract reasoning is built from physical metaphors: CONTAINER (in/out), PATH (source-goal), FORCE (push/resistance), UP-DOWN, CENTRE-PERIPHERY. "Building an idea" is not a dead metaphor. It reflects actual cognitive architecture.

**Lawrence Barsalou** - Perceptual Symbol Systems (1999). Concepts are grounded in sensorimotor simulations, not abstract symbols. When you think "chair," your brain partially simulates sitting.

**John Hench** - Designing Disney (2003). Kinesthetic design: the way a path curves, narrows, or opens creates emotional responses the body registers even when the mind doesn't. Colour as emotion. Movement through space as narrative.

**Imagineering's Concept Development phase** - Every concept includes sensory specifications. Not "a warm environment" but "indirect amber lighting at 2700K, oak panelling with visible grain, the sound of water at 40dB from the left."

## What Changes

Without this principle: "The onboarding should feel welcoming."

With this principle: "The first screen is warm - off-white, not clinical white. The font has weight but not heaviness. The animation eases in like a door opening slowly. No sound. Just the visual equivalent of a deep breath."

## The Rule

Specific or nothing. Generic sensory language is worse than none. It creates the illusion of embodiment without the substance.
